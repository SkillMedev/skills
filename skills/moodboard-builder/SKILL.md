# Moodboard Builder

A moodboard is not a Pinterest dump - it is a curated argument for one specific visual direction, made before pixels are pushed. The costly failure mode is a board of 40 nice-looking images that lets every stakeholder see whatever they want in it, so "alignment" evaporates the moment real design work appears. This procedure produces a tight board and a written statement that survive the client meeting.

## Operating procedure

Order matters: the emotional target is the filter every image must pass through, so writing it after sourcing guarantees a board justified backwards.

### Step 1: Gather inputs

Collect before sourcing anything. Where the user does not know, use the default and label the guess as a guess.

- What the brand does and who the primary customer is (no default - ask).
- Project stage: new brand, rebrand, or refresh (changes how far the board may stray from existing equity; default: new brand).
- Where the direction will live first: web product, packaging, social, environments (default: web product).
- Existing constraints: locked colors, existing logo, category conventions that cannot be broken (default: none).
- Audience for the board itself: internal creative team or paying client (a client board needs more annotation; default: client).

### Step 2: Write the emotional target

Before sourcing a single image, write two or three sentences on how the brand should make someone feel when they encounter it - not what the brand does, how it feels. Example: "Confident without arrogance. Warm like a trusted expert, not warm like a lifestyle brand. Technical precision expressed through simplicity." Every image is judged against this filter, and only this filter.

### Step 3: Source wide, then cut to 8-16

Gather 30-50 candidates fast, then cut ruthlessly to 8 to 16 images - never more. Each survivor must earn its place by contributing something no other image on the board does: a color mood, a texture, a compositional principle, a lighting style, a typographic reference, a photography approach. Cutting rules:

- If two images say the same thing, keep the stronger one. No duplicates of a point.
- Remove anything that is merely nice-looking but says nothing specific.
- No more than 2 images from the same brand or campaign, or the board reads as "copy them".

### Step 4: Organize by visual dimension

Structure the board so a viewer can read each of these five dimensions without explanation. Every dimension needs at least one image carrying it:

1. Color and tone - what hues and value ranges dominate.
2. Texture and material feel - smooth/rough, digital/analog, flat/tactile.
3. Light and shadow - harsh/soft, high-contrast/diffuse.
4. Composition and space - dense/airy, centered/dynamic, symmetrical/asymmetrical.
5. Human presence - if people appear: who they are and how they are portrayed.

### Step 5: Label inspiration vs. execution

Tag every image as either directional inspiration (sets mood, will not be literally reproduced) or execution reference (close to what will actually be made). A fashion editorial might set the mood while the actual photography will be product-led; a vintage poster might establish texture while the brand uses a modern grid. Skipping this step causes clients to expect the literal references rather than the principles they embody.

### Step 6: Build the anti-board

Add 3 to 4 images showing directions to avoid, each with a one-line reason. "Not this" is often clearer than "this", and it protects against the client instinct to request elements from the rejected category once design begins.

### Step 7: Write the art direction statement

Accompany the board with a written statement of 150 to 300 words. It names the references, explains why they were chosen, and translates visual choices into verbal principles a photographer, illustrator, or motion designer can follow without seeing the board. It also gives stakeholders evaluation language: "does this feel more like Reference A or Reference B?"

## Template: art direction statement

```
ART DIRECTION - [FILL: brand name]

Emotional target: [FILL: 2-3 sentences on how the brand should feel]

Direction in one line: [FILL: e.g. "clinical warmth - lab precision softened by human light"]

Color and tone: [FILL: dominant hues, value range, what is deliberately absent]
Texture and material: [FILL]
Light: [FILL]
Composition: [FILL: grid behavior, density, whitespace stance]
People: [FILL: who appears, how portrayed - or "no human presence, because ..."]

Inspiration references (mood, not literal): [FILL: refs + one line each]
Execution references (close to final output): [FILL: refs + one line each]

Not this: [FILL: 3-4 rejected directions, one line each on why]

Test for future work: "Does this feel more like [FILL: ref A] or [FILL: ref B]?
It should always feel like [FILL: A or B]."
```

## Deliverable

Produce a moodboard of 8-16 images organized by the five visual dimensions, each image tagged inspiration or execution, a 3-4 image anti-board with reasons, and a completed 150-300 word art direction statement using the template above.

## Do NOT

- Do not exceed 16 images. Above that, the board stops being an argument and becomes a survey; stakeholders each align to a different subset.
- Do not source before the emotional target is written - images chosen first get a rationale invented afterwards.
- Do not mix inspiration and execution without labels; this is the single most common cause of "this isn't what the moodboard promised" disputes.
- Do not fill a dimension with a weak image just to check the box; state "texture is intentionally neutral" instead.
- Do not present the board without the written statement - the board aligns eyes, the statement aligns decisions made weeks later without the board open.

## Quality bar

Before presenting, verify:

- Every image passes the emotional-target filter and contributes something unique.
- All five dimensions are readable from the board without narration.
- The statement is 150-300 words and usable by a photographer who never sees the board.
- The anti-board exists and each rejection has a stated reason.
- A stranger shown the board would describe the direction in words close to the emotional target.

When the direction is approved and needs codifying into rules, hand off to brand-guidelines; for building the palette in detail, pair with color-palette-builder.
