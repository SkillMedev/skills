# Donor Communications

Most small nonprofits contact individual donors only when they want money, and then wonder why second gifts never come - when the sector-wide reality is that fewer than half of first-time donors ever give again, and the cheapest revenue any nonprofit has is a retained donor. This skill builds the communication system that keeps donors giving: segmentation, fast gratitude, impact stories, disciplined appeals, and a calendar that earns the right to ask.

Work the example organization throughout: Bright Futures Tutoring, a youth-education nonprofit with a $420,000 annual budget, 3 staff, serving 250 students. Individual donors provide roughly $120,000 of the budget alongside the grant pipeline (see grant-prospect-researcher). Its outcome numbers are the canonical ones from the pack's logic model: 72 percent of students gained a grade level in reading last year - the same figure impact-report-builder publishes.

## Operating procedure

### Step 1: Segment the donor file

Four segments, four treatments. Sort every donor into exactly one:

- Major: the top of the file - for an organization Bright Futures' size, gifts of $1,000+ or the top 10 percent of donors. Treatment: personal. Handwritten or personally signed thanks, a phone call from the ED for gifts at this level, one-to-one updates, invited to see the program in person.
- Recurring: monthly or committed annual givers. Treatment: protect the relationship - never resolicit them in general appeals as if their gift were invisible; send an annual "your $25/month did this" cumulative-impact note.
- First-time: gave once, within the last 12 months. The riskiest segment. Treatment: a distinct welcome path - thank-you, then a no-ask impact story within 30 days, before any second appeal.
- Lapsed: no gift in 13+ months (13, not 12, so annual givers on a yearly rhythm are not prematurely flagged). Treatment: a "we miss you" message pairing their last gift's impact with one concrete update. For the full multi-touch re-engagement sequence - timing, channel order, escalating offers - route to win-back-campaign; this skill defines who is lapsed and what the message says, win-back-campaign runs the mechanics.

### Step 2: Install the thank-before-you-bank rule

Gratitude within 48 hours of every gift, before the deposit is even processed if possible. The thank-you is the single highest-leverage communication in fundraising: prompt, specific thanks measurably outperforms slow, generic receipts for retention. Requirements: sent within 48 hours; names what the gift does in program terms ("$150 funds a semester of small-group tutoring for one student"); contains no ask and no "while we have your attention" postscript; signed by a person, not "The Team." The IRS receipt language can ride along, but the letter is a thank-you that contains a receipt, not a receipt pretending to be a thank-you.

### Step 3: Build impact stories with the donor as hero

Structure, in order: one beneficiary (a single named-with-permission or anonymized student - one, because a reader can care about Maya but not about "250 students"); concrete change (Maya went from two grade levels behind to reading at grade level, one specific before/after, drawn from the same outcomes the logic model reports); donor-as-hero framing (the donor's gift made it possible - "you put a tutor across the table from Maya twice a week," not "our innovative program achieved"). The organization is the guide; the donor is the protagonist. Close with the aggregate line for scale: "Maya is one of 250 students - and 72 percent of them gained at least a grade level in reading this year."

### Step 4: Write appeals with the standard anatomy

Appeal-letter anatomy, in order: (1) open with a story or a jolting local fact - never "Dear Friend, as the year draws to a close"; (2) the problem, local and concrete; (3) what the organization does about it, with the headline outcome; (4) the specific ask - a dollar amount tied to a unit of impact ("$150 = a semester of tutoring for one student; $600 = a full year"), because untied round numbers depress response; (5) urgency that is real (a match deadline, a program-year start), never manufactured; (6) a P.S. that restates ask and deadline - it is the second-most-read line of any letter. Segment the ask amounts: anchor each donor at or slightly above their last gift, never below it.

### Step 5: Build the communication calendar on the 3:1 ratio

The ratio, defined here for the pack: at least three impact touches for every one ask. A touch is anything that gives (story, outcome update, event invite, thank-you); an ask is anything that requests money. Bright Futures' year at roughly monthly cadence: 2-3 appeals (year-end, spring, optionally a fall match) and 8-9 impact touches - program-launch update, mid-year story, the annual report from impact-report-builder, a back-to-school note, a "you did this" video, family-night photos, and the thank-yous riding on every gift. Year-end matters disproportionately: a large share of individual giving lands in November-December, so the calendar protects that window with impact touches in October and the appeal in late November.

## Inputs to collect

Donor file with gift dates and amounts (a spreadsheet is fine at this scale), current retention rate if known (label a guess as a guess; if unknown, assume the sector-typical 40-45 percent overall and worse for first-timers), the canonical outcome numbers (from the logic model - do not restate them differently here), one strong beneficiary story with permission status, and staff hours available per month for donor work.

Good/bad thank-you contrast - Bad: "Thank you for your generous donation. Your support helps us fulfill our mission." Good: "Your $150 gift puts a tutor across the table from one student, twice a week, for an entire semester. Last year 72 percent of those students gained at least a grade level in reading. That's what you just did." Specific unit of impact, real outcome number, donor as actor.

## Deliverable

A segmented donor grid with treatment rules, a 48-hour thank-you template per segment, one donor-as-hero impact story, one appeal letter following the anatomy, and a 12-month communication calendar that visibly holds the 3:1 ratio.

## Do NOT

- Do not send the same message to all segments; a major donor treated like a mailing-list entry becomes a lapsed major donor.
- Do not let a thank-you carry an ask; gratitude with a price tag reads as neither.
- Do not tell aggregate stories ("we served 250 students") when one beneficiary is available; scale belongs in the closing line, not the lede.
- Do not make the organization the hero of its own appeal; the donor funds the story, so the donor stars in it.
- Do not invert the 3:1 ratio under revenue pressure - over-asking is how files burn out, and rebuilding a burned file costs more than the appeals raised.
- Do not use outcome numbers that differ from impact-report-builder's; a donor who reads both notices.

## Quality bar

- Every donor sits in exactly one segment with a defined treatment.
- Thank-you turnaround is 48 hours and the letter names a unit of impact.
- Every story has one beneficiary, one concrete change, donor-as-hero verbs.
- The calendar shows the 3:1 ratio holding for every segment.
- Outcome figures match the logic model in grant-proposal-writer and the report in impact-report-builder exactly.
