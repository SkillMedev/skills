# Comparison Page Builder

Build a comparison page that wins "X vs Y" and "best alternative to X" searches by being genuinely fair, because a shopper who catches one false claim about a rival distrusts every claim about you.

## Workflow
1. Gather inputs: your product, the named competitor(s), the buyer segment you serve best, and the real, verifiable differences. If the requester cannot supply verifiable differences, stop and ask — do not invent them.
2. Concede at least one real point: name something the competitor genuinely does better. Honest concession is what makes every other claim believable; "we win on everything" reads as marketing and converts worse.
3. Build the table as the page centerpiece, placed within the first scroll. Pick 6–10 criteria that drive the buying decision (price, key features, support, warranty, ease of use, fit for use case), not vanity specs. One short value, phrase, or checkmark per cell. Attribute and date competitor figures; where a value is unknown, write "varies" or omit the cell — never fabricate a number.
4. Frame by use case: add "Choose us if…" and "Choose them if…" blocks above or below the table. This routes the right buyer in, the wrong-fit buyer out (fewer refunds, better retention), and captures "X vs Y for [use case]" long-tail intent.
5. Optimize for the query: use natural "X vs Y" phrasing in the H1, title, and intro; answer the implicit "which is better" question in the first paragraph — a direct 40–60 word answer is the format featured snippets favor; add an FAQ of 4–6 "Is X or Y better for…" questions; link to the relevant product page with descriptive anchor text.
6. Run the legal/ethics check before shipping: every comparative claim is substantiated, accurate, and dated; no disparagement, no misleading trademark use, no stale pricing. Put a re-verification date on the calendar — competitor pricing and plans should be re-checked at least quarterly, and a table whose sources are more than 6 months old is a liability, not an asset.

## Deliverable

Produce a publish-ready comparison page containing:

1. **The comparison table** — 6–10 decision-driving criteria, one value per cell, every competitor figure sourced and dated.
2. **The routing copy** — a direct first-paragraph answer to "which is better" (40–60 words), plus "Choose us if…" and "Choose them if…" blocks naming distinct buyer segments, including at least one honest concession.
3. **The SEO scaffolding** — H1, title tag, and meta description using literal "X vs Y" phrasing; a 4–6 question FAQ; internal links to the relevant product page with descriptive anchors.
4. **A claims register** — each comparative claim with its source and verification date, plus the scheduled quarterly re-check date.

## Quality bar
- Every competitor data point is verifiable and carries a source or date; no fabricated specs or prices.
- At least one genuine competitor advantage is stated plainly.
- Both "Choose us if…" and "Choose them if…" blocks are present and route distinct buyer segments.
- The H1, title, and first paragraph all use the literal "X vs Y" phrasing and answer the buyer's question directly.
- The table sits within the first scroll and no source behind it is older than a quarter without re-verification.

## Do NOT
- Do not misstate a competitor's features or hide your product's weaknesses — refuse if asked; it destroys trust and invites legal exposure.
- Do not fill the table with vanity specs that do not affect the buying decision.
- Do not force a head-to-head your product loses on the criteria most searchers care about; recommend a niche-angle page on a use case you genuinely win instead.
- Do not ship undated or unsourced competitor pricing and specs.
- Do not bury the table below the fold under brand storytelling — the searcher came for the comparison; make them scroll past marketing and they bounce.
- Do not let the page rot: a comparison quoting a rival's discontinued plan or old pricing is the false claim that poisons the rest of the page.
- Do NOT use when building a single-product landing page, PDP, or generic marketing copy with no named rival, or an internal feature-comparison spec — those are not commercial-intent "vs" pages.
