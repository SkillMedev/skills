# Seller Lead Nurture

Listings are won 6-18 months before the sign goes in the yard: by the time a homeowner interviews agents, most have already decided who they trust, and the interview is a formality. The costly mistake this skill prevents is competing at the listing appointment instead of before it — the agent who has been usefully answering "what is my home worth" for a year does not get compared, they get called.

Worked example throughout: Priya, a residential agent in a suburban market, 14 transactions last year at a $485,000 average sale price, building toward 24. Her seller pool: past clients, homeowners in her farm area, and the neighbors captured at open houses (the community lane from open-house-follow-up — the most-missed source). At her $485k average, each listing won from this pool is a five-figure commission; 4-5 additional listings a year is roughly half the gap between 14 and 24 transactions, and listings compound because each yard sign and open house generates the next round of leads.

## Operating procedure

1. **Build the pool and tag each entry** with address, purchase year and price if knowable, relationship (past client / farm / open-house neighbor / referral), and any stated timeline. Public records give purchase year and price for most entries.
2. **Start the quarterly home-value-update cadence** — the backbone touch, running before anything fancier, because consistency beats cleverness in a 12-month game.
3. **Send the equity-position letter** to entries with meaningful tenure (5+ years in the home) once per year.
4. **Wire the lifecycle triggers** — these are the event-driven touches that convert; the calendar cadence just keeps permission alive between events.
5. **Offer the full CMA as the conversion event** whenever a trigger produces engagement — and route the actual analysis and presentation to cma-narrative-builder.
6. **Run the listing-appointment prep checklist** when the appointment is booked.

## Inputs to collect

- The pool: past clients (start here — highest trust, lowest volume), the farm (pick one geographic area the agent can dominate rather than three they cannot), and open-house neighbor captures.
- Per entry: address, approximate purchase year/price (public record; label estimates as estimates), email/mobile with consent status, relationship tag.
- Local data feeds: recent solds by street, median price trend, days on market for the area.
- The agent's realistic capacity: a farm of 200-400 homes is workable solo; 1,000 is a mailing list, not a relationship.

## The quarterly home-value-update touch

One personal-feeling email (or letter for the farm) per entry, four times a year:

```
Subject: Your [FILL: street name] home value — [FILL: season] update

Hi [FILL: name] — quick update on your side of the neighborhood.
Homes near you sold between $[FILL] and $[FILL] this quarter, and the
median for [FILL: area] moved [FILL: direction/amount] since last update.
Based on public data, your home is likely in the $[FILL: band, e.g.
470-500k] range — that's an estimate from the outside; a real number
takes 30 minutes and a walkthrough, free, whenever useful.
No pressure either way — I'll keep the updates coming.
```

Rules: always a band, never a single number (a false-precision number from the curb is a credibility trap and invites anchoring you will have to fight later in cma-narrative-builder); always the soft CMA offer; never "thinking of selling?" pressure — the update IS the value.

## The equity-position letter (annual)

For owners with 5+ years of tenure, translate appreciation into a personal number:

```
You bought in [FILL: year] at $[FILL: purchase price]. Similar homes in
[FILL: area] have been selling around $[FILL: current band]. Net of a
typical remaining mortgage balance for a [FILL: year] purchase, your
equity position is likely in the $[FILL: range] neighborhood. That's
money that can move — toward a bigger home, a smaller one, or an
investment property — whenever the timing is right for you.
```

This letter outperforms the generic value update because it is about THEIR balance sheet, not the market. Label every derived figure as an estimate. Never claim knowledge of their actual mortgage balance — say "typical remaining balance for a purchase of that vintage."

## Lifecycle triggers

Event-driven touches convert; send within 48 hours of the event:

- **Neighborhood sale** — the highest-converting trigger: "A home on your street just sold — [FILL: address] closed at $[FILL]. That sale moves the data for your home; want an updated number?" When 742 Alder Court closes at its $499,000-list sale, every entry on Alder Court and adjacent streets gets this within two days.
- **Listing anniversary / purchase anniversary**: "Five years in the house this month — congrats. Here's what's changed in the market since you bought." Pairs naturally with the equity letter.
- **Their engagement**: opened the last three updates, clicked the value link, replied anything → personal note, not another automated touch.
- **Life-event signals the agent learns humanly** (job change, addition planned, kids leaving) → a conversation, never an automated blast referencing personal information.
- **Visible market shift in their band** (inventory drought, DOM drop): "Homes like yours are selling in under two weeks right now — sellers' leverage is unusually good."

## The CMA offer as conversion event

Every touch carries a low-pressure path to the same conversion: a free, no-obligation, in-person CMA. Accepting the CMA is the moment a nurture entry becomes a seller lead — the walkthrough is the first listing appointment whether either side names it that. From acceptance onward, cma-narrative-builder owns the comp selection, the pricing story, and the objection scripts; this skill's job is done when the appointment is booked and prepped.

## The listing-appointment prep checklist

- [ ] Full CMA built per cma-narrative-builder (comps defended, price band set, search-bracket number chosen, Zillow script rehearsed)
- [ ] Pull their nurture history — what they clicked and asked; walk in knowing their curiosity trail
- [ ] The property's public record reviewed: purchase price/year, permits, lot
- [ ] Net-proceeds worksheet drafted at the band (they will ask "what do I walk away with" — have it)
- [ ] Marketing plan one-pager: where the listing copy runs (listing-description-writer), open-house plan (open-house-follow-up), portal syndication
- [ ] Two recent seller references ready
- [ ] The one question that wins appointments prepared: "What does the move make possible for you?" — motivation shapes everything downstream, including negotiation posture later in re-negotiation-prep

## Thresholds

- Cadence floor: 4 value touches per year per entry, plus triggers. Below quarterly, the pool goes cold and re-warming costs more than maintaining.
- Trigger speed: neighborhood-sale touches within 48 hours of closing data appearing.
- Healthy annual conversion: 3-6 percent of a maintained pool lists within 24 months (past clients convert far above farm entries). Below 2 percent, audit whether touches carry real numbers or generic fluff.
- CMA-offer-to-appointment: above 60 percent of accepted CMA offers should become in-person walkthroughs — the offer language may be too casual otherwise.

## Deliverable

A running seller pipeline: a tagged pool with quarterly value updates scheduled, annual equity letters for tenured owners, lifecycle triggers wired to local sale data, every touch carrying the CMA offer, and a prep checklist that turns booked appointments into signed listings via cma-narrative-builder.

## Do NOT

- Do not send a single-number valuation from the curb — bands only, until the walkthrough.
- Do not open any touch with "thinking of selling?" — pressure converts a permission asset into an unsubscribe.
- Do not farm three areas thinly instead of one deeply — density is what makes "a home on your street just sold" literally true.
- Do not automate touches that reference personal life events — those are conversations.
- Do not discard open-house neighbors — they are pre-warmed farm entries who have already met the agent.
- Do not describe the neighborhood's people in any touch ("desirable families are moving in") — market facts, sale prices, and property data only; demographic commentary is a fair-housing violation.
- Do not walk into the listing appointment without the CMA done — "I'll get you numbers next week" hands the listing to the agent who came prepared.

## Quality bar

- Every entry has a tag, a next scheduled touch, and consent status.
- Every value touch contains at least two real local numbers and a band, never a point estimate.
- Every equity figure is labeled an estimate with its assumptions stated.
- Neighborhood-sale triggers fire within 48 hours.
- Every booked appointment has the full prep checklist complete.

## Escalation and compliance

Real estate is a licensed profession. All claims must be accurate and estimates labeled; fair-housing rules prohibit steering and demographic targeting language — value updates and triggers cite sale prices, days on market, and property facts, never characterizations of who lives in or is moving into an area. Mail and electronic touches must honor consent, opt-outs, and any state rules on solicitation of owners already under listing agreements (never solicit a listed property). Legal and brokerage compliance review applies to all templates. Handoffs: the CMA conversion event routes to cma-narrative-builder; buyer inquiries that surface from this pool route to buyer-nurture-sequence; once the listing is signed, listing-description-writer writes the copy and offers are handled in re-negotiation-prep.
