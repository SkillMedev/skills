# Client Onboarding System

The first 14 days of an engagement set the operating norms for its entire life: who communicates where, how fast things move, and whether the client experiences the freelancer as a professional operation or an improvising individual. The costly mistake this skill prevents is the drift start - a signed contract followed by two weeks of scattered emails, missing assets, and no visible output, which trains the client to micromanage before any work has shipped.

Run this immediately after the SOW from statement-of-work-writer is signed and the deposit or first retainer invoice has cleared. The cadence established here is what client-status-reporting runs on from week 2 onward. Worked example: Maya, a freelance brand designer, onboarding her first Brand System Retainer client ($4,900/month, up to 36 hours) - a DTC hot-sauce brand.

## Operating procedure

### Step 1: Collect the inputs

1. The signed SOW: deliverables, cadence, and the designated approver.
2. Payment confirmation. No onboarding before the first invoice clears - starting work on promise-to-pay undoes the payment discipline set in statement-of-work-writer.
3. The client-side cast: approver, day-to-day contact, and anyone whose assets or access are needed.
4. A candidate first quick win (see Step 5) - pick it before kickoff so the first week has a target. Label it a guess until the client confirms priorities.

### Step 2: Send the pre-kickoff package (day 1)

Within one business day of payment, send one email containing: the kickoff call invite (within 5 business days of signing), the asset/access checklist (Step 3) so collection starts before the call, and a one-paragraph "how we'll work together" preview of the communication contract. This email is the first proof the operation is systematic.

### Step 3: Run the asset and access collection checklist

Missing assets are the number-one cause of week-one stalls, and every stall in week one reads as the freelancer being slow. Send as a single checklist with one named client-side owner and a due date before or at the kickoff call:

```
ASSETS & ACCESS - [FILL: client name]     Owner: [FILL]   Due: [FILL: date]

BRAND MATERIALS
[ ] Logo source files (AI/EPS) and any existing brand guide
[ ] Fonts and licenses; brand color values if defined
[ ] Product photography (highest resolution available)
[ ] Past design work worth keeping or explicitly retiring

ACCESS
[ ] Shared drive or DAM folder (edit access)
[ ] [FILL: platforms per SOW - e.g. social accounts, web CMS, print vendor contacts]
[ ] Billing/PO contact if invoicing needs a purchase order

CONTEXT
[ ] One-page brief: business goals this quarter, launch dates, competitors
[ ] The designated approver confirmed by name
```

Chase at 48 hours before due; at kickoff, any unchecked item gets a named owner and date on the call. Track blockers in the "blocked" section of client-status-reporting thereafter.

### Step 4: Run the kickoff call (within 5 business days, 45-60 minutes)

Fixed agenda, sent in advance:

1. **Introductions and roles** (5 min): confirm approver and day-to-day contact - one approver, per the SOW.
2. **Outcome replay** (10 min): restate the engagement outcome in the client's words and what "great" looks like at 90 days. Misalignment surfaces here, when it is cheap.
3. **Scope boundaries** (10 min): walk the SOW inclusions AND exclusions aloud. Reading exclusions out loud feels awkward and prevents the "I assumed that was included" conversation later - scope-creep-defense works far better when this moment happened.
4. **Communication contract** (10 min): agree the terms in Step 6 explicitly.
5. **First quick win and 30-day plan** (15 min): confirm the week-1 deliverable and the first month's priorities.
6. **Asset checklist review** (5 min): assign owners and dates to anything missing.

Send a recap the same day: decisions, owners, dates. The recap is the first artifact of the reporting habit.

### Step 5: Apply the first-quick-win rule

**Ship something visible in week 1.** Not a plan, not a questionnaire - a piece of real work the client can see and ideally use. It must be inside SOW scope, achievable without the slowest missing assets, and small enough to survive the 2 standard revision rounds within the week. Maya's pick: a brand consistency audit of the hot-sauce brand's Instagram plus 2 corrected social templates - real Tier 2 scope, needs only public material and the logo files, and shows taste immediately. The quick win converts the client's post-signing anxiety ("did I just commit $4,900/month to the right person?") into confirmation, and it buys patience for the slower structural work behind it.

### Step 6: Set the communication contract

Agree these in writing at kickoff - defaults below, adjustable, but never left implicit:

- **Channels**: one async channel (email or the client's Slack) for requests and approvals; requests made in passing on calls get restated in the channel to count. No SMS/WhatsApp for work requests.
- **Response times**: freelancer responds within 1 business day; this is a professional-services norm, not an emergency line. Anything needing same-hour response is outside standard scope.
- **Working hours**: stated explicitly (e.g. Mon-Fri 9-6 client's timezone). Messages outside hours are fine to send, answered next business day.
- **Meeting cadence**: per the SOW tier (Maya: one 60-minute monthly creative direction call) plus the weekly written status report from client-status-reporting. Extra standing meetings are a scope change.
- **Feedback**: consolidated comments from the single approver, within 5 business days, matching the SOW's revision and deemed-acceptance terms.

### Step 7: Close out day 14

By day 14: assets collected or every gap has an owner and date; quick win shipped and reacted to; first status report sent; communication contract holding (and gently enforced once if tested - the first off-channel 10pm request gets a friendly redirect, which is when the norm becomes real).

## Concrete thresholds

- Kickoff within 5 business days of signing; pre-kickoff package within 1 business day of payment.
- Quick win shipped by end of week 1 - visible work, not planning artifacts.
- Asset checklist due at or before kickoff; chase at 48 hours; unresolved items become named blockers.
- Response-time norm: 1 business day; feedback window: 5 business days (matching statement-of-work-writer).
- One designated approver. Two approvers means doubled revision rounds in practice.

## Deliverable

A completed onboarding record: sent pre-kickoff package, filled asset checklist, kickoff recap with decisions and owners, the written communication contract, and the shipped week-1 quick win - plus the first weekly status report, handing the engagement to client-status-reporting as its ongoing operating system.

## Do NOT

- Do not start onboarding before payment clears; it silently renegotiates the SOW's payment terms on day one.
- Do not let week 1 produce only plans and questionnaires - invisible work reads as no work.
- Do not skip reading the exclusions aloud at kickoff to avoid awkwardness; that discomfort is the cheapest scope defense available.
- Do not accept "whoever's around" as the approval process; extract one named approver.
- Do not answer the first late-night off-channel message at speed; every exception in week 1 becomes policy by week 4.

## Quality bar

Onboarding passes when: kickoff happened on time with the recap sent same-day; the communication contract exists in writing with all five elements; the quick win shipped inside week 1 within SOW scope; and every missing asset has an owner and a date.

## Neighbors

statement-of-work-writer comes before this skill (nothing here should contradict the signed SOW). client-status-reporting takes over from day 14. When the first out-of-scope request arrives mid-onboarding - it often does - use scope-creep-defense.
