# Testimonial Capture Interview

A testimonial that says "great product, great team!" persuades no one, and it is not the customer's fault — it is the interviewer's, because vague questions produce vague praise. The costly mistake this skill prevents is burning a willing customer's only interview slot on questions that yield nothing quotable, then either publishing mush or ghost-writing a quote the customer never said. A well-sequenced interview reliably extracts the before/after story with numbers, and the ethics workflow makes every published word both true and approved.

## Operating procedure

The sequence matters most inside the interview itself (Step 3): specifics come before feelings because memory works that way — a customer asked "how do you feel about the product" gives adjectives, but a customer walked through their old workflow first gives numbers, and the feelings that follow attach to those numbers.

### Step 1: Select and brief the customer

Pick customers with a measurable outcome and at least 90 days of usage — earlier than that, the "after" hasn't stabilized and the numbers won't survive scrutiny. Send a short brief when scheduling: the purpose (a story about their results, in their words), the time ask (30 minutes), the control they keep (nothing publishes without their written approval of the exact words), and a request to have any relevant numbers handy. The approval promise up front measurably improves both acceptance and candor.

### Step 2: Prepare the specifics you already know

Before the call, pull what the account already shows — usage data, support history, the original sales notes on why they bought. Interviewing from zero wastes half the slot on facts you had; interviewing from data lets you ask "your team went from 40 to 300 workflows in Q2 — what happened there?", which is where good soundbites come from.

### Step 3: Run the question sequence

Record the call (with consent, stated at the start — recording consent is separate from publication consent and both are required). Then run five phases in order:

1. **Before — the old world (5 min).** "Walk me through how you handled [job] before us." "What did that cost you — time, money, headcount, missed deadlines?" "What finally made you look for something new?" Push for the day-in-the-life texture; the "before" pain is what makes the "after" credible.
2. **Decision (3 min).** "What alternatives did you look at?" "What nearly stopped you from choosing us?" The hesitation quote is marketing gold because prospects share the same hesitation.
3. **After — the new world, with numbers (10 min).** The core. "What does that same workflow look like now?" Then chase every vague claim to a number with the two magic follow-ups: **"Can you give me a specific example?"** and **"Roughly how much — even a ballpark?"** "It's much faster" becomes "reports that took a day now take twenty minutes." Accept ranges and labeled estimates; never accept adjectives as the final answer.
4. **Feelings and stakes (5 min).** Only now: "What would going back feel like?" "What surprised you?" "What would you tell someone in your old situation?" Emotion after evidence produces the quotable line that carries a number's weight behind it.
5. **Logistics (2 min).** Confirm name/title/company attribution preferences, ask whether their numbers can be published or need anonymizing ("a 40 percent reduction" vs the raw figures), and restate the approval workflow.

### Step 4: Extract the soundbites

Within 24 hours (memory of tone decays fast), process the transcript:

1. Highlight every sentence containing a number, a named specific, or a strong verb.
2. From those, pull 5–10 candidate soundbites of one to two sentences each — a soundbite must stand alone without the surrounding conversation.
3. Tag each by job: **outcome quote** (the number), **hesitation-overcome quote** (objection handling), **switch quote** (why they left the old way), **recommendation quote** (the tell-a-friend line).
4. Rank by the specificity test: could a competitor's customer have plausibly said this exact sentence? If yes, it ranks low.

### Step 5: Edit within the ethics boundary

The rule is **tighten, never fabricate**. Permitted: removing filler ("um," "you know," false starts), fixing grammar the speaker would want fixed, condensing two adjacent sentences into one, and trimming with ellipses that do not change meaning. Forbidden: adding words the customer did not say, strengthening a claim ("faster" does not become "10x faster" without their number), combining sentences from different topics into one implied statement, and changing what any statement means. When a quote needs a fact the customer implied but didn't state cleanly, write the candidate version and ask them to confirm it as theirs — never publish your paraphrase as their words.

### Step 6: Run the permission and approval workflow

1. Send the edited quotes — exact final wording, with attribution line and where they will appear — to the customer in writing.
2. Ask for written approval (email suffices) of the specific wording, not a blanket "can we quote you."
3. If their company requires PR or legal sign-off, route it through your contact and budget 1–2 weeks; enterprise logos usually require this.
4. Log approvals: quote, approver, date, permitted uses. Re-approval is required if wording later changes or the use expands materially (a website quote does not automatically license a billboard).
5. Honor removal requests without argument — a burned reference costs more than any quote is worth.

## Inputs to collect

- Which customer, and the measurable outcome hypothesis (default: pull the three accounts with the strongest usage growth and label the outcome a hypothesis until the customer confirms it).
- Account data available before the call: usage metrics, tenure, original purchase driver.
- Where the material will be used — website, case study, sales deck, ads — because the approval must cover the actual uses.
- Recording tooling and consent script.
- Whether numbers can be published raw or need anonymizing (default: ask the customer; when in doubt, percentages over absolutes).

## Concrete thresholds

- Customer tenure ≥ 90 days before interviewing for outcomes.
- Interview length 25–30 minutes; the after-with-numbers phase gets the largest share (~10 min).
- Soundbite yield: a good interview produces 5–10 candidates; fewer than 3 with a number or named specific means re-interview or pick a different customer — do not pad with mush.
- Process the transcript within 24 hours.
- Written approval of exact wording before any publication, no exceptions; enterprise sign-off budget 1–2 weeks.
- Specificity test on every published quote: it must contain at least one element (number, named workflow, timeframe) that a stranger could not have invented.

## Worked artifact — interview script

```
TESTIMONIAL INTERVIEW SCRIPT — [FILL: customer name, company] — [FILL: date]
Consent: "I'd like to record this so I can quote you accurately — nothing
gets published without your written approval of the exact words. OK?"

BEFORE (5 min)
- Walk me through how you handled [FILL: the job] before [FILL: product].
- What did that cost you — time, money, people, missed deadlines?
- What finally made you go looking for something different?

DECISION (3 min)
- What else did you consider?
- What nearly stopped you from choosing us?

AFTER — NUMBERS (10 min)
- What does that same workflow look like today?
- [On any vague claim] Can you give me a specific example?
- [On any claim without a number] Roughly how much — even a ballpark?
- Your account shows [FILL: data point from Step 2] — what's the story there?

FEELINGS (5 min)
- What would going back feel like?
- What surprised you most?
- What would you tell someone still doing it the old way?

LOGISTICS (2 min)
- Attribution: name, title, company OK? Numbers publishable or anonymized?
- Next step: I'll send you the exact quotes for written approval — nothing
  runs without it.
```

**Contrast pair — what the sequence produces:**

Bad (feelings-first interview): *"We love the product. The team is fantastic and it's made a huge difference for us."* — unusable; says nothing a competitor's customer couldn't say.

Good (specifics-first interview): *"Month-end reporting used to take my team three full days. Last quarter we closed the books in one afternoon — I got Friday back, every month."* — an outcome, a number, a human stake, and only this customer could have said it.

## Deliverable

An interview package: the recording and transcript, 5–10 tagged and ranked soundbites edited within the ethics boundary, the attribution and anonymization decisions, and the written-approval log — raw material ready to hand to case-study-builder.

## Do NOT

- **Do NOT ask "how do you like the product?" as an opener.** It produces adjectives; the before-walkthrough produces evidence.
- **Do NOT accept a vague claim as final.** Every "much better" gets the specific-example and ballpark follow-ups; unquantified praise is the interview failing, not succeeding.
- **Do NOT fabricate, strengthen, or splice quotes.** A customer who finds words in their mouth they never said is a lost account and a public risk; "tighten, never fabricate" has no exceptions.
- **Do NOT publish on verbal enthusiasm.** "Sure, quote me!" on a call is not approval of specific wording in writing.
- **Do NOT interview customers under 90 days in.** Honeymoon quotes lack outcomes and age badly.
- **Do NOT skip recording consent** or conflate it with publication consent — they are separate permissions.

## Quality bar

- Every published quote passes the specificity test and traces to the transcript.
- At least 3 soundbites carry a number or named concrete outcome.
- Edits verifiably within the tighten-never-fabricate boundary (diff the published quote against the transcript).
- Written approval logged for the exact final wording and the actual uses.
- Attribution and anonymization match the customer's stated preference.

## Escalation and neighbors

Hand the package to case-study-builder to construct the narrative asset; this skill ends at approved raw material. For interviews whose goal is learning rather than marketing — expert input, discovery research — use expert-interview, which optimizes for information instead of quotability. Claims in regulated industries (health outcomes, financial returns) may need legal review before publication regardless of customer approval — route those to counsel.
